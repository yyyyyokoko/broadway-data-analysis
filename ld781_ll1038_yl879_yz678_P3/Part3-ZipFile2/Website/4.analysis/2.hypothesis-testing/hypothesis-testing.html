Hypothesis Testing

Is there any seasonal trend in ticket sold?

Hypothesis:

Seats sold during Thanksgiving week, winter holiday week, and summer break months spike as opposed to the rest of the time during the year.

Method: one-way ANOVA analysis

Data Preparation: categorize date into different time periods

Results: p value = 0.016, which indicates that there is a difference in the mean amount of seats sold among the 4 groups.

A post-hoc analysis was applied to detect which 2 groups have differences in mean.

The seats sold over summer breaks are the lowest and those of Thanksgivings are the highest. According to the pairwise analysis, the only significant result is the difference between summer break and thanksgiving.

Our initial hypothesis was partially supported. Thanksgiving is indeed a profitable season for Broadway. But conversely, summer breaks sold less tickets, which was not as expected. Thus, summer time might be interpreted as a low season of Broadway shows.

Do money makers also have high ratings?

Ratings vs. Average Weekly Grosses of Recent 5 Years

We can observe an overall positive relationship between ratings and average weekly grosses. There are a few shows that deviate from the majority. For example, Hamilton has a rating of 7.44 and an extremely high average weekly gross of $2.6M and Penn and Teller On Broadway has extreme low rating of 2.75, but a relatively high average weekly gross of $1.3M.

Method: Logistic Regression

Data Preparation

Merged grosses and ratings data; limited the time scope to be the recent 5 years; a high rating is defined as a score greater than 7; data normalization

Results: The correlation coefficient is 2.98 and the p-value is 0.009.

We have also realized that only incorporating one factor into the model leaves other factors uncontrolled, therefore weakening the explanatory power of our model.

Is the size of a theater correlated with its ticket price?

the Gershwin Theater

The largest theater on Broadway (1,933 seats)

the Helen Hayes Theater

The smallest theater on Broadway (597 seats)

Method: Linear Regression

Data Preparation:

Calculate theater sizes: # of seats = (# of weekly seats sold / % of cap) / # of performances per week; log transformation to adjust the skewness; control for seasonality

Results:

A correlation coefficient of 0.1805, with a p-value close to zero, indicating a positive relationship between the theater size and the average ticket price.
